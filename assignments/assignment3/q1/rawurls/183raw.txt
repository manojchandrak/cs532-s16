      By <a href="/news/tom-fedorowytsch/5825102" target="_self" title="">Tom Fedorowytsch</a></div>
  



  
    <p class="published">
      Updated 
    <span class="timestamp">
      February 11, 2016 08:13:56
    </span>
    </p>
  
  

<div class="attached-content">
      <div class="inline-content photo left">
    <a href="/news/2016-02-11/elise-duffield-and-sally-amazon-with-tadhg/7158038">











  
  
  
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
  <img src="http://www.abc.net.au/news/image/7157812-3x2-340x227.jpg" alt="Elise Duffield (left) and Sally Amazon (right) with Tadhg and other family members" title="Elise Duffield left and Sally Amazon right with Tadhg and other family members" width="340" height="227"/>
</a><a href="/news/2016-02-11/elise-duffield-and-sally-amazon-with-tadhg/7158038" class="inline-caption"><strong>
        Photo:</strong>
       Elise Duffield (L) and Sally Amazon (R) with young Tadhg and other family members at Parliament, where they saw the bill pass. <span class="source">(Courtesy: Susan Close)</span>
      </a></div>
<div class="inline-content map left">
      <div class="story-map"></div>
      <a class="inline-caption" href="http://maps.google.com/?q=-34.9295,138.5982(Adelaide%205000)&amp;z=5">
        <strong>Map: </strong>
        Adelaide 5000</a>
    </div>
  </div>
  <p>A rule that prevented both females in a same-sex relationship<strong> </strong>from being recognised on their children's birth certificates will be changed in South Australia after passage of a bill through both houses of State Parliament.</p><div class="inline-content wysiwyg right">
<div class="inner">
  <h2>Key points:</h2><ul><li>Same-sex parents were required to prove they had lived together for three years for both to be listed on birth certificates</li><li>MLC Tammy Franks says bill changing that rule was slow to get through SA's Parliament </li><li>Bill passed Lower House in conscience vote 29-12</li></ul></div>
</div><p> </p><p>Elise Duffield gave birth to a son Tadhg on Mother's Day in 2014, but a rule prevented her partner Sally Amazon from also being on the birth certificate.</p><p>The rule required the couple to prove they had lived together for three years, a time frame which would not be applied to heterosexual couples who were able to legally marry.</p><p>"I read the fine print, it said we had to have lived together for three years before conception, which we hadn't," Ms Duffield said.</p><p>Ms Amazon agreed the couple could have lied, but said they chose not to.</p><p>Ms Amazon said she had been worried her lack of legal status as Tadhg's parent would later become an issue and was pleased the legislative change had made its way through the Parliament.</p><p>"It means a tremendous relief — we've been in legal limbo since Tadhg was born," she said.</p><p>"We planned together to have this baby and had no expectation we were going to have any problems registering as co-parents.</p><p>"We've just been crossing our fingers that we haven't had any traumas in our lives that meant that legal limbo caused us any problems."</p><p>Ms Amazon said it was good news for her entire family, not just young Tadhg.</p><p>"It also means that his siblings who live with him will be his legal siblings," she said.</p><p>"He has a whole side of the family that was invisible."</p><p>Ms Duffield shared the relief that their campaign for equality for Tadhg was all but over now.</p><p>"It's been a long time coming, he's 22 months old [this week]," she said.</p><h2>Small issue of greater importance, MLC says</h2><p>Greens MLC Tammy Franks steered the bill through the state Upper House last June, and sat with Tadhg and his parents as it passed the Lower House on Wednesday.</p><p>"Most people in the community couldn't understand what the fuss was about or why it was taking so long," Ms Franks said.</p><p>"It's been incredibly offensive to see this very small issue, that's so important to a very small number of people, stretch out over almost a year of debate."</p><div class="inline-content photo right">
    <a href="/news/2016-02-11/tammy-franks-of-the-greens.jpg/7158050">











  
  
  
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
  <img src="http://www.abc.net.au/news/image/4948814-3x2-340x227.jpg" alt="Tammy Franks" title="Tammy Franks of the Greens" width="340" height="227"/>
</a><a href="/news/2016-02-11/tammy-franks-of-the-greens.jpg/7158050" class="inline-caption"><strong>
        Photo:</strong>
       Tammy Franks is critical the bill took so long to get through both Houses. <span class="source">(ABC News)</span>
      </a></div><p> </p><p>The bill passed the Lower House in a conscience vote 29-12.</p><p>Liberal MP Adrian Pederick said feedback from people in his electorate had been entirely negative.</p><p>"I think it also brings about some problems in regards to a child having an ability to track their genealogy later in life," he said.</p><p>Attorney-General John Rau was supportive of the bill's intent but expressed concern about its timing, ahead of a broader review of discriminating laws in South Australia.</p><p>"We should wait until they've finished and done a comprehensive review before we start doing piecemeal moves," he said.</p><p>But the bill passed with support across party lines, including from Liberal MP David Pisoni and Labor MP Katrine Hildyard.</p><p>Ms Franks said she remained keen to see more rights achieved for same-sex couples.</p><p>"The public supports same-sex marriage, the politicians are still a bit scared but they should realise there's nothing to be afraid of," she said.</p><p>Once the legislation is rubber-stamped by the Upper House, Tadhg's parents will be able to apply for his birth certificate to be re-issued.</p>
