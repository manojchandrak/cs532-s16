<p class="p1"><span class="KonaFilter image-container display-table float_left float_right"><span><span data-post-image class="image on-image"><img src="http://static6.techinsider.io/image/569ea060c08a80492c8b8f4b-4272-2848/shutterstock_348317801.jpg" alt="iPhone apps and headphones" data-mce-source="Shutterstock"><span class="source"><span>Shutterstock</span></span><span class='caption'>Make your iPhone more zen with the Kondo method.</span></span><p></span></span>Marie Kondo is a Japanese lifestyle guru and author of "<a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1607747308/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=1607747308&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=bivideo-20&amp;linkId=5EXCPRCMUY3NWIVR%20%20"><span class="s1">The Life-Changing Magic of Tidying Up: The Japanese Art of Decluttering and Organizing</span></a>.” She's known for helping people decrease clutter and straighten up their homes.</p>
<p class="p1">But it turns out <a href="http://www.techinsider.io/picture-of-kondoed-homes-inspired-by-marie-kondo-2015-7">her method</a> — which involves holding each item you own in your hands and asking if it gives you joy — could also help us declutter our electronic devices.</p>
<p class="p2">Smartphones have rid of us of a lot of clutter, such as notepads, recorders, calendars, and recipe cards.</p>
<p class="p2">But that doesn’t mean your phone itself can’t get messy, especially with myriad notifications popping up on your screen or hundreds of apps to download.</p>
<p class="p4"><span class="s2">The Wall Street Journal’s Christopher Mims realized he could use Kondo’s method on his phone and wrote about it in his article “<a href="http://www.wsj.com/articles/the-magic-of-tidying-updigitally-1437338785">The Magic of Tidying Up — Digitally</a>.” </span><span class="s2"><a href="http://www.wnyc.org/story/infomagical-challenge-2/">Speaking with </a></span><a href="http://www.wnyc.org/story/infomagical-challenge-2/">WNYC Studios’ </a><a href="http://www.wnyc.org/shows/notetoself/"><span class="s3">Note to Self</span></a> podcast team, Mims described how he would click on each app and ask himself if it gave him joy. If not, he’d promptly delete it and move on to another app and another app until he had gone through his entire phone.</p>
<p class="p1"><strong><a href="http://www.techinsider.io/picture-of-kondoed-homes-inspired-by-marie-kondo-2015-7">Kondo herself told Business Insider</a> you can tell when something sparks joy when you "feel your body go upward."</strong> If something doesn't make you happy when you touch it, Kondo said you should "thank it for its service" and get rid of it, or in this case, delete the app from your phone.</p>
<p class="p4">After he deleted all of his unnecessary apps, Mims would then place all of his remaining apps into one folder and forget about them until he needed them.</p>
<p class="p4"><span class="KonaFilter image-container display-table"><span><span data-post-image class="image on-image"><img src="http://static1.techinsider.io/image/55faaedfdd08956d3a8b45de-800-600/ipad%20folders.jpg" alt="iPad folders" data-mce-source="MacRumors"><span class="source"><span>MacRumors</span></span><span class='caption'>Keep all your apps in one searchable place.</span></span><p></span></span></p>
<p class="p5">"By putting all of [your] apps into folders, you can search for them by name [using the Spotlight tool in your iPhone],” Mims told Note to Self. “What happens is your device becomes task-oriented, instead of the place [where] you go to be like, 'OK, what do I need to do next?'"</p>
<p class="p8">By shedding unnecessary apps that he wasn’t using, Mims said he was left with 82 apps. That may seem like a lot, but 80 is actually the average number of apps the typical iPhone user has, <a href="http://thenextweb.com/apple/2012/03/05/apples-app-store-users-install-80-apps-per-device-each-app-downloaded-45454-times-on-average/#gref">according to Apple</a>.</p>
<p class="p6">But the “Kondo”-ing of his phone didn’t stop there. Mims also suggests turning off notifications on apps when they aren’t necessary. That way instead of seeing thousands of red notification markers, you won’t see any.</p>
<p class="p1">Can you imagine how zen that would be? Here's what my homescreen looks like after going through the process. To be honest, it's a little scary.</p>
<p class="p1"><span class="KonaFilter image-container display-table"><span><span data-post-image class="image on-image"><img src="http://static1.techinsider.io/image/56b20c7d6e97c630008b4665-750-1334/img_5909.jpg" alt="zen phone screen" data-mce-source="Megan Willett/Tech Insider"><span class="source"><span>Megan Willett/Tech Insider</span></span><span class='caption'>No notifications for me.</span></span><p></span></span></p>
<p class="p1">According to Kondo herself, using her method will surround you with the things that you love and give you more storage than you thought possible, something that also applies to our digital life.</p>
<p class="p1">Kondo for her part is flattered that Mims uses her method like this.</p>
<p class="p1">“It’s very nice,” she told Note to Self. “When you tidy and organize things using my method, it’s very important that you only keep things that spark joy for you, but also to keep it simple, so this is a very good way to do that.”</p>
<p class="p1"><a href="http://www.wnyc.org/story/infomagical-challenge-2/">You can listen to the entire podcast here</a>.</p>
