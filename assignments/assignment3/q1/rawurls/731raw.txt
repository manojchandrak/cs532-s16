<p> David Cameron tried to defend his Government's record on housing and homelessness today at <a data-content-type="live-event" href="http://www.mirror.co.uk/news/uk-news/pmqs-live-david-cameron-faces-7343577">Prime Minister's Questions</a> - but his figures don't stand up to scrutiny. </p> <p> Labour leader <a data-action="jeremy-corbyn" data-content-type="section-topic" data-track="false" data-type="inline" data-word-count="2" href="http://www.mirror.co.uk/all-about/jeremy-corbyn">Jeremy Corbyn</a> skewered him with a series of tough questions on his forthcoming housing bill. </p> <p>He asked a question from a member of the public called Rosie, who is stuck living with her parents because she can't afford to rent a home of her own.</p> <p>The PM largely ignored Rosie's plight, instead reeling off a string of ropey statistics.</p> <p> But look into the figures and many of <a href="http://mirror.co.uk/all-about/david-cameron">Mr Cameron's</a> claims quickly fall apart. </p> <p> Here's four times <a data-action="david-cameron" data-content-type="section-topic" data-track="false" data-type="inline" data-word-count="2" href="http://www.mirror.co.uk/all-about/david-cameron">David Cameron</a> misled MPs over the Conservative Party's record on housing. </p> <h3>1. &quot;Homelessness is half the peak that it was under Labour&quot;</h3> <p> <figure class="inline-image clearfix ">
<img src="http://i4.mirror.co.uk/incoming/article7343958.ece/ALTERNATES/s615b/PMQs-5.jpg"
width="615"

alt=""

<figcaption>Mr Cameron implied homelessness was on the way down - but is it?

</p> <p> <a data-action="david-cameron" data-content-type="section-topic" data-track="false" data-type="inline" data-word-count="2" href="http://www.mirror.co.uk/all-about/david-cameron">David Cameron</a> claimed that homelessness is less than half the peak it was under the last Labour Government. </p> <p>While it's true that homelessness reached its peak at 115,270 in 2004/05 while Labour were in power, that figure dropped dramatically year after year.</p> <p> That is, until 2010, when <a data-action="david-cameron" data-content-type="section-topic" data-track="false" data-type="inline" data-word-count="2" href="http://www.mirror.co.uk/all-about/david-cameron">David Cameron</a> became Prime Minister. </p> <p>Since then, it's risen every year but one - and has shot up by 55% since the Tories swept into number 10.</p> <p> <!-- start of widget id: 3230031, name: charts, view: barGraph -->

</p> <h3>2. The bar for what counts as being homeless has been raised</h3> <p> <figure class="inline-image clearfix ">

</p> <p>The homelessness statistics in the early 2000s were undeniably quite high - but all is not quite as it seems.</p> <p>At the time, single men in overcrowded households were counted among the homelessness figures.</p> <p>The criteria for homelessness has changed since then, with overcrowding removed from the stats.</p> <p> The bar for what counts as being homeless has been raised - and it's <em>still</em> going up under the Tories. </p> <p> <figure class="inline-image clearfix ">

</p> <h3>4. And he was talking about council houses, not social housing</h3> <p> <figure class="inline-image clearfix ">
<div class="image-credit-wrapper">

</p> <p> Mr Cameron likes to trump up his government's record on building <em>council</em> housing - because it's a nice, easy soundbite. </p> <p>But the figure is almost meaningless in the full context of social housing in the UK - the bulk of social housing is being built by Housing Associations.</p> <p>The graph below shows the total number of social houses being built, broken down into council and Housing Associations.</p> <p> Some 328,000 <em>social</em> houses were built under the last Labour government, compared to 161,000 between 2010 and May this year.</p> <p>So far, the Tories have been building at roughly the same rate (or slightly slower) than Labour were in the last years of their Government.</p> <p></p> <p> <figure class="inline-image clearfix ">
