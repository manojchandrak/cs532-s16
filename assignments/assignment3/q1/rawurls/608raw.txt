                          <p>The former diving coach at <a title="Towson University" href="/topic/education/colleges-universities/towson-university-OREDU0000148-topic.html">Towson University</a> pleaded guilty Wednesday to placing a cellphone in a locker room and secretly recording members of a team that once considered her to be a &quot;second mom.&quot;</p><p>Maureen Mead, 43, received three years of probation before judgment, which would allow her record to eventually be cleared if she meets all conditions.</p><div class="trb_gptAd trb_ar_main_ad" data-role="adloader adloader_ad delayload delayload_done" data-state="adloader_unloaded"  data-withinviewport-options="bottomOffset=100&topOffset=1000000"  data-adloader-position="articleCube" data-adloader-size="300x250" data-adloader-adtype="articlebottomcube"  ></div

  <p>In a plea agreement with prosecutors, Mead pleaded guilty in Baltimore County Circuit Court to visual surveillance &#8212; also known as &quot;peeping Tom&quot; &#8212; and altering physical evidence in connection to the October incident. Prosecutors dropped a felony wiretap charge.</p><p>Mead, who was fired, is married to former Towson head swimming coach Pat Mead, who resigned in December.</p><span class="trb_ar_cont" data-ar-cont="Article continues below"></span>

It was not until a team member's mother called a dean at the school that Towson University campus police were called to investigate &#8212; about four hours after the phone was found.</p><p>By the time police seized the phone, it had been &quot;factory reset,&quot; Dever said &#8212; with all information erased.</p><p>Two days later, Pat Mead called assistant coach Adrienne Phillips, met her off school grounds and told her she should reset her phone, Dever said. He also told her that he and his wife had reset their phones and iPads.</p><p>Dever said the women did not believe their coach's intent was to record them getting undressed, but rather to spy on them talking. Team members had even joked before that they thought they were being recorded because the Meads knew things from private conversations.</p><p>Dever argued for a three-year suspended sentence, saying the swimmers had suffered a betrayal of trust that was &quot;heart-wrenching.&quot; The coaching staff downplayed the incident, making the women feel worse, she said.</p><p>&quot;They dedicate everything &#8212; their heart and their soul goes into it,&quot; Dever said of the swimmers. &quot;They would've walked the ends of the earth for their coaches.&quot;</p><p>Alexander heard emotional testimony from seven women who described how Maureen Mead's actions affected them. Some said Mead had once felt like a &quot;second mom.&quot;</p><p>The betrayal of trust shook their sense of identity, they said. One woman said she felt sick to her stomach going to the pool after the phone incident. Several women described feeling paranoid about being recorded in the locker room, once considered a private place.</p><p>Mead, a former teacher, briefly addressed the court and apologized. A mother of four, she said she has lost her reputation and career. She also wished the women luck in their future swimming careers.</p><p>&quot;I never intentionally would hurt any of them,&quot; she said.</p><p>She has struggled to find a new job because of the charges, defense attorney David Irwin said.
