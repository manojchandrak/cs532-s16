  <div class="content"><p><a href="https://ourfiniteworld.com/2016/02/08/the-physics-of-energy-and-the-economy/"><em>Submitted by Gail Tverberg via Our Finite World blog,</em></a></p>
<p>I approach the subject of the physics of energy and the economy with some trepidation. An economy seems to be a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissipative_system">dissipative system</a>, but what does this really mean? <strong><em>There are not many people who understand dissipative systems, and very few who understand how an economy operates</em></strong>. The combination leads to an awfully lot of false beliefs about the energy needs of an economy.</p>
<p><strong>The primary issue at hand is that, as a dissipative system, every economy has its own energy needs, just as every forest has its own energy needs (in terms of sunlight) and every plant and animal has its own energy needs, in one form or another</strong>. A hurricane is another dissipative system. It needs the energy it gets from warm ocean water. If it moves across land, it will soon weaken and die.</p>
<p>There is a fairly narrow range of acceptable energy levels&ndash;an animal without enough food weakens and is more likely to be eaten by a predator or to succumb to a disease. A plant without enough sunlight is likely to weaken and die.</p>
<p>In fact, the effects of not having enough energy flows may spread more widely than the individual plant or animal that weakens and dies. If the reason a plant dies is because the plant is part of a forest that over time has grown so dense that the plants in the understory cannot get enough light, then there may be a bigger problem. The dying plant material may accumulate to the point of encouraging forest fires. Such a forest fire may burn a fairly wide area of the forest. Thus, the indirect result may be to put to an end a portion of the forest ecosystem itself.</p>
<p><strong>How should we expect an economy to behave over time? </strong>The pattern of energy dissipated over the life cycle of a dissipative system will vary, depending on the particular system. In the examples I gave, the pattern seems to somewhat follow what Ugo Bardi calls a Seneca Cliff.</p>
<div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_40416" style="width: 549px;"><em><a href="https://gailtheactuary.files.wordpress.com/2015/11/senecacliff.jpg" rel="attachment wp-att-40416"><img alt="Figure 1. Seneca Cliff by Ugo Bardi" class="size-full wp-image-40416" src="https://gailtheactuary.files.wordpress.com/2015/11/senecacliff.jpg?w=640" style="height: 387px; width: 599px;" /></a></em><br />
<p class="wp-caption-text"><em>Figure 1. Seneca Cliff by <a href="http://cassandralegacy.blogspot.com/2011/08/seneca-effect-origins-of-collapse.html">Ugo Bardi</a></em></p>
</div>
<p>The Seneca Cliff pattern is so-named because long ago,&nbsp;<a href="http://cassandralegacy.blogspot.com/2011/08/seneca-effect-origins-of-collapse.html">Lucius Seneca wrote</a>:</p>
<blockquote><div class="quote_start"><div></div></div><div class="quote_end"><div></div></div><p>It would be some consolation for the feebleness of&nbsp;<span class="GINGER_SOFTWARE_mark" id="6b0623d8-6465-44da-b69c-523914183fbd">our selves</span>&nbsp;and our works if all things should perish as slowly as they come into being; but as it is, increases are of sluggish growth, but the way to ruin is rapid<em>.</em></p>
</blockquote>
<p><u><strong>The Standard <em>Wrong</em> Belief about the Physics of Energy and the Economy</strong></u></p>
<p>There is a standard <strong>wrong</strong> belief about the physics of energy and the economy; it is the belief we can somehow train the economy to get along without much energy.</p>
<p><strong>In this wrong view, the only physics that is truly relevant is the thermodynamics of oil fields and other types of energy deposits.</strong> All of these fields deplete if exploited over time. Furthermore, we know that there are a finite number of these fields. Thus, based on the Second Law of Thermodynamics, the amount of free energy we will have available in the future will tend to be less than today. This tendency will especially be true after the date when &ldquo;peak oil&rdquo; production is reached.</p>
<p><em><strong>According to this wrong view of energy and the economy, all we need to do is design an economy that uses less energy. We can supposedly do this by increasing efficiency, and by changing the nature of the economy to use a greater proportion of services. </strong></em>If we also add renewables (even if they are expensive) the economy should be able to get along fine with very much less energy.</p>
<p><strong>These wrong views are amazingly widespread. </strong>They seem to underlie the widespread hope that the world can reduce its fossil fuel use by 80% between now and 2050 without badly disturbing the economy.&nbsp;The book&nbsp;<a>2052: A Forecast for the Next 40 Years</a>&nbsp;by Jorgen Randers seems to reflect these views. Even the &ldquo;Stabilized World Model&rdquo; presented in the 1972 book <a href="http://www.donellameadows.org/wp-content/userfiles/Limits-to-Growth-digital-scan-version.pdf">The Limits to Growth</a>&nbsp;by Meadow et al. seems to be based on naive assumptions about how much reduction in energy consumption is possible without causing the economy to collapse.</p>
<p><u><strong>The Economy as a Dissipative System</strong></u></p>
<p>If an economy is a dissipative system, it needs sufficient energy flows. Otherwise, it will collapse in a way that is analogous to animals succumbing to a disease or forests succumbing to forest fires.</p>
<p>The primary source of energy flows to the economy seems to come through the leveraging of human labor with supplemental energy products of various types, such as animal labor, fossil fuels, and electricity. For example, a man with a machine (which is made using energy products and operates using energy products) can make more widgets than a man without a machine. A woman operating a computer in a lighted room can make more calculations than a woman who inscribes numbers with a stick on a clay tablet and adds them up in her head, working outside as weather permits.</p>
<p>As long as the quantity of supplemental energy supplies keeps rising rapidly enough, human labor can become increasingly productive. This increased productivity can feed through to higher wages. Because of these growing wages, tax payments can be higher. Consumers can also have ever more funds available to buy goods and services from businesses. Thus, an economy can continue to grow.</p>
<p><strong>Besides inadequate supplemental energy, the other downside risk to continued economic growth is the possibility that diminishing returns will start making the economy less efficient. </strong>These are some examples of how this can happen:</p>
<ul>
<li>Deeper wells or desalination are needed for water because aquifers deplete and population grows.</li>
<li>More productivity is needed from each acre of arable land because of growing population (and thus, falling arable land per person).</li>
<li>Larger mines are required as ores of high mineral concentration are exhausted and we are forced to exploit less productive mines.</li>
<li>More pollution control devices or higher-cost workarounds (such as &ldquo;renewables&rdquo;) are needed as pollution increases.</li>
<li>Fossil fuels from cheap-to-extract locations are exhausted, so extraction must come from more difficult-to-extract locations.</li>
</ul>
<p>In theory, even these diminishing returns issues can be overcome, if the leveraging of human labor with supplemental energy is growing quickly enough.</p>
<p><strong>Theoretically, technology might also increase economic growth. The catch with technology is that it is very closely related to energy consumption. </strong>Without energy consumption, it is not possible to have metals. Most of today&rsquo;s technology depends (directly or indirectly) on the use of metals. If technology makes a particular type of product cheaper to make, there is also a good chance that more products of that type will be sold. Thus, in the end, growth in technology tends to allow more energy to be consumed.</p>
<p><u><strong>Why Economic Collapses Occur</strong></u></p>
<p><strong>Collapses of economies seem to come from a variety of causes.</strong> One of these is inadequate wages of low-ranking workers (those who are not highly educated or of managerial rank). This tends to happen because if there are not enough energy flows to go around, it tends to be the wages of the &ldquo;bottom-ranking&rdquo; employees that get squeezed. In some cases, not enough jobs are available; in others, wages are too low.&nbsp;This could be thought of as inadequate return on human labor&ndash;a different kind of low Energy Return on Energy Invested (<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_returned_on_energy_invested">EROEI</a>) than is currently analyzed in most of today&rsquo;s academic studies.</p>
<p>Another area vulnerable to inadequate energy flows is the price level of commodities. If energy flows are inadequate, prices of commodities will tend to fall below the cost of producing these commodities. This can lead to a cutoff of commodity production. If this happens, debt related to commodity production will also tend to default. Defaulting debt can be a huge problem, because of the adverse impact on financial institutions.</p>
<p><strong>Another way that inadequate energy flows can manifest themselves is through the falling profitability of companies, such as the falling revenue that banks are now experiencing.</strong> Still another way that inadequate energy flows can manifest themselves is through falling tax revenue. Governments of commodity exporters are particularly vulnerable when commodity prices are low. Ultimately, these inadequate energy flows can lead to bankrupt companies and collapsing governments.</p>
<p><strong>The closest situation that the US has experienced to collapse is the Depression of the 1930s. </strong>The Great Recession of 2007-2009 would represent a slight case of inadequate energy flows&ndash;one that could be corrected by a large dose of Quantitative Easing (QE)(leading to the lower cost of borrowing), plus debt stimulus by China. These helped bring oil prices back up again, after they fell in mid-2008.</p>

<p><strong>Clearly, we are now again beginning to experience the effects of inadequate energy flows. This is worrying, because many economies have collapsed in the past when this situation occurred.</strong></p>
<p><u><strong>How Energy Flows of an Economy are Regulated</strong></u></p>
<p>In an economy, the financial system is the regulator of the energy flows of the system. If the price of a product is low, it dictates that a small share of energy flows will be directed toward that product. If it is high, it indicates that a larger share of energy flows will be directed toward that product. Wages follow a similar pattern, with low wages indicating low flows of energy, and high wages indicating higher flows of energy. Energy flows in fact &ldquo;pay for&rdquo; all aspects of the system, including more advanced technology and the changes to the system (more education, less time in the workforce) that make advanced technology possible.</p>
<p><strong>One confusing aspect to today&rsquo;s economy is the use of a &ldquo;pay you later&rdquo; approach to paying for energy flows.</strong> If the energy flows are inadequate using what we would think of as the natural flows of the system, debt is often used to increase energy flows. Debt has the effect of directing <em>future</em> energy flows in a particular direction, such as paying for a factory, a house, or a car. These flows will be available when the product is already part of the system, and thus are easier to accommodate in the system.</p>
<p><strong>The use of increasing debt allows total &ldquo;demand&rdquo; for products of many kinds to be higher, because it directs both <em>future flows</em> and <em>current flows</em> of energy toward a product. </strong>Since factories, houses and cars are made using commodities, <em>the use of an increasing amount of debt tends to raise commodity prices</em>. With higher commodity prices, more of the resources of the economy are directed toward producing energy products. This allows for increasing energy consumption. This increased energy consumption tends to help flows of energy to many areas of the economy at the same time: wages, taxes, business profitability, and funds for interest and dividend payments.</p>
<p><strong>The need for debt greatly increases when an economy begins using fossil fuels, because the use of fossil fuels allows a step-up in lifestyle.</strong> There is no way that this step-up in lifestyle can be paid for in advance, because the benefits of the new system are so much better than what was available without fossil fuels. For example, a farmer raising crops using only a hoe for a tool will never be able to save up sufficient funds (energy flows) needed to pay for a tractor.&nbsp;While it may seem bizarre that banks loan money into existence, this approach is in fact <em>essential</em>, if adequate energy flows are to be available to compensate for the better lifestyle that the use of fossil fuels makes possible.</p>
<p><strong>Debt needs are low when the cost (really energy cost) of producing energy products is low. Much more debt is needed when the cost of energy extraction is high. </strong>The reason more debt is needed is because fossil fuels and other types of energy products tend to leverage human labor, making human labor more productive, as mentioned previously. In order to maintain this leveraging, an adequate <em>quantity</em> of energy products (measured in British Thermal Units or Barrels of Oil Equivalent or some similar unit) is needed.</p>
<p><strong>As the required price for energy-products rises, it takes ever-more debt to finance a similar amount of energy product, plus the higher cost of homes, cars, factories, and roads using the higher-cost energy.&nbsp;</strong>In fact, with higher energy costs, capital goods of all kinds will tend to be more expensive. This is a major reason why the ratio of debt to GDP tends to rise as the cost of producing energy products rises. At this point, in the United States it takes approximately $3 of additional debt to increase GDP by $1 (author&rsquo;s calculation).</p>

<p>Clearly<strong> one of the risk factors to an economy using fossil fuels is that debt levels will become unacceptably high.</strong></p>
<p><strong>A second risk is that debt will stop rising fast enough to keep commodity prices at an acceptably high level. </strong>The recent slowdown in the growth of debt (Figure 3) no doubt contributes to current low commodity prices.</p>
<p><strong>A third risk to the system is that the rate of economic growth will slow over time because even with the large amount of debt added to the system</strong>, the leveraging of human labor with supplemental energy will not be sufficient to maintain economic growth in the face of diminishing returns. In fact, it is clearly evident that US economic growth has trended downward over time (Figure 4).</p>

<p><strong>A fourth risk is that the whole system will become unsustainable.</strong> When new debt is issued, there is no real matching with future energy flow. For example, will the wages of those taking on debt to pay for college be sufficiently high that the debtors can afford to have families and buy homes? If not, their lack of adequate income will be one of the factors that make it difficult for the prices of commodities to stay high enough to encourage extraction.</p>
<p>One of the issues in today&rsquo;s economy is that promises of future energy flows extend far beyond what is formally called <em>debt</em>. These promises include shareholder dividends and payments under government programs such as Social Security and Medicare. Reneging on promises such as these is likely to be unpopular with citizens. Stock prices are likely to drop, and private pensions will become unpayable. Governments may be overthrown by disappointed citizens.</p>
<h2><u><strong>Examples of Past Collapses of Economies</strong></u></h2>
<p><u><strong>Example of&nbsp;the Partial Collapse of the Former Soviet Union</strong></u></p>
<p>One recent example of a partial collapse was that of the Former Soviet Union (FSU) in December 1991. I call this a partial collapse, because it &ldquo;only&rdquo; involved the collapse of the central government that held together the various republics. The governments of the individual republics remained in place, and many of the services they provided, such as public transportation, continued. The amount of manufacturing performed by the FSU dropped precipitously, as did oil extraction. Prior to the collapse, the FSU had serious financial problems. Shortly before its collapse, the world&rsquo;s leading industrial nations <a href="http://forums.philosophyforums.com/threads/why-did-the-soviet-union-collapse-54320-15.html">agreed to lend the Soviet Union $1 billion and defer repayment on $3.6 billion more in debt.</a></p>
<p>A major issue that underlay this collapse was a fall in oil prices to the $30 per barrel range in the 1986 to 2004 period. The Soviet Union was a major oil exporter. The low price had an adverse impact on the economy, a situation similar to that of today.</p>
<div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_40618" style="width: 650px;"><a href="https://gailtheactuary.files.wordpress.com/2016/02/fsu-oil-production-and-price-2014.png" rel="attachment wp-att-40618"><img alt="Figure 4. Oil production and price of the Former Soviet Union, based on BP Statistical Review of World Energy 2015. " class="size-full wp-image-40618" src="https://gailtheactuary.files.wordpress.com/2016/02/fsu-oil-production-and-price-2014.png?w=640&amp;h=384" style="width: 600px; height: 360px;" /></a><br />
<p class="wp-caption-text"><em>Figure 5. Oil production and price of the Former Soviet Union, based on BP Statistical Review of World Energy 2015.</em></p>
</div>
<p>Russia continued to pump oil even after the price dropped in 1986. In fact, it raised oil production, to compensate for the low price (energy flow it received per barrel). This is similar to the situation today, and what we would expect if oil exporters are very dependent on these energy flows, no matter how small. Oil production didn&rsquo;t fall below the 1986 level until 1989, most likely from inadequate funds for reinvestment. Oil production rose again, once prices rose.</p>
<p>Figure 6 shows that the FSU&rsquo;s consumption of energy products started falling precipitously in 1991, the year of the collapse&ndash;very much a Seneca Cliff type of decline.</p>
<div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_39913" style="width: 650px;"><a href="https://gailtheactuary.files.wordpress.com/2015/06/former-soviet-union-energy-consumption-by-fuel-2014.png" rel="attachment wp-att-39913"><img alt="Figure 5. Former Soviet Union energy consumption by source, based on BP Statistical Review of World Energy Data 2015." class="size-full wp-image-39913" src="https://gailtheactuary.files.wordpress.com/2015/06/former-soviet-union-energy-consumption-by-fuel-2014.png?w=640&amp;h=386" style="width: 600px; height: 362px;" /></a><br />
<p class="wp-caption-text"><em>Figure 6. Former Soviet Union energy consumption by source, based on BP Statistical Review of World Energy Data 2015.</em></p>
</div>
<p>In fact, consumption of all fuels, even nuclear and hydroelectric, fell simultaneously. This is what we would expect if the FSU&rsquo;s problems were caused by the low prices it was receiving as an oil exporter. With low oil prices, there could be few good-paying jobs. Lack of good-paying jobs&ndash;in other words, inadequate return on human labor&ndash;is what cuts demand for energy products of all kinds.</p>
<p>A drop in population took place as well, but it didn&rsquo;t begin until 1996. The decrease in population continued until 2007. Between 1995 and 2007, population dropped by a total of 1.6%, or a little over 0.1% per year. Before the partial collapse, population was rising about 0.9% per year, so the collapse seems to have reduced the population growth rate by about 1.0% per year.&nbsp;Part of the drop in population was caused by excessive alcohol consumption by some men who had lost their jobs (their sources of energy flows) after the fall of the central government.</p>
<p>When commodity prices fall below the cost of oil production, it is as if the economy is cold because of low energy flows. Prof. Francois Roddier describes the point at which collapse sets in as the point of<em> self-organized criticality</em>. According to Roddier (personal correspondence):</p>
<div class="page" title="Page 14">
<div class="layoutArea">
<div class="column">
<blockquote><div class="quote_start"><div></div></div><div class="quote_end"><div></div></div>
<p>Beyond the critical point, wealth condenses into two phases that can be compared to a gas phase and a liquid phase. A small number of rich people form the equivalent of a gas phase, whereas a large number of poor people form what corresponds to a liquid phase. Like gas molecules, rich people monopolize most of the energy and have the freedom to move. Embedded in their liquid phase, poor people have lost access to both energy and freedom. Between the two, the so-called middle class collapses.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>I would wonder whether the ones who die would be equivalent to the solid state. They can no longer move at all.</p>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><u><strong>Analysis of Earlier Collapses</strong></u></p>
<p>A number of studies have been performed analyzing earlier collapses. Turchin and Nefedov in <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Secular-Cycles-Peter-Turchin/dp/0691136963">Secular Cycles</a> analyze eight pre-fossil fuel collapses in detail. Figure 7 shows my interpretation of the pattern they found.</p>
<div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_38150" style="width: 650px;"><a href="https://gailtheactuary.files.wordpress.com/2013/05/shape-of-typical-secular-cycle.png" rel="attachment wp-att-38150"><img alt="Figure 7. Shape of typical Secular Cycle, based on work of Peter Turkin and Sergey Nefedov in Secular Cycles." class="size-full wp-image-38150" src="https://gailtheactuary.files.wordpress.com/2013/05/shape-of-typical-secular-cycle.png?w=640&amp;h=384" style="width: 600px; height: 360px;" /></a><br />
<p class="wp-caption-text"><em>Figure 7. Shape of typical Secular Cycle, based on work of Peter Turchin and Sergey Nefedov in <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Secular-Cycles-Peter-Turchin/dp/0691136963">Secular Cycles</a>.</em></p>
</div>
<p>Again, the pattern is that of a Seneca Cliff. Some of the issues leading to collapse include the following:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Rising population relative to farmland</strong>. Either farmland was divided up into smaller plots, so each farmer produced less, or new workers received &ldquo;service&rdquo; type jobs, at much reduced wages. The result was falling earnings of many non-elite workers.</li>
<li><strong>Spiking food and energy prices.&nbsp;</strong>Prices were high at times due to lack of supply, but held down by low wages of workers.</li>
<li><strong>Rising need for government to solve problems</strong> (for example, fight war to get more land; install irrigation system so get more food from existing land). Led to a need for increased taxes, which impoverished workers could not afford.</li>
<li><strong>Increased number of nobles and high-level administrators.&nbsp;</strong>Result was increased disparity of wages.</li>
<li><strong>Increased debt,</strong> as more people could not afford necessities.</li>
</ol>
<p>Eventually, the workers who were weakened by low wages and high taxes tended to succumb to epidemics. Some died in wars. Again, we have a situation of low energy flows, and the lower wage workers not getting enough of these flows. Many died&ndash;in some cases as many as 95%. These situations were much more extreme than those of the FSU. On the favorable side, the fact that there were few occupations back in pre-industrial days meant that those who did survive could sometimes resettle with other nearby communities and continue to practice their occupations.</p>
<p>Joseph Tainter in <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Collapse-Complex-Societies-Studies-Archaeology/dp/052138673X">The Collapse of Complex Societies</a> talks about the need for increasing complexity, as diminishing returns set in. This would seem to correspond to the need for increased government services and an increased role for businesses. Also included in increased complexity would be increased hierarchical structure. All of these changes would leave a smaller share of the energy flows for the low-ranking workers&ndash;a problem mentioned previously.</p>
<p><a href="https://translate.googleusercontent.com/translate_c?depth=1&amp;hl=en&amp;prev=search&amp;rurl=translate.google.com&amp;sl=fr&amp;u=https://www.youtube.com/watch%3Fv%3DddmQhIiVM48&amp;usg=ALkJrhgeMjnxuS6-qVRPwcwSA9TXcRGROw">Dr. Tainter also makes the point</a> that to maintain complexity, &ldquo;Sustainability may require greater consumption of resources, not less.&rdquo;</p>
<p><u><strong>A Few Insights as to the Nature of the Physics Problem</strong></u></p>
<p>The&nbsp;<a href="http://www.livescience.com/50941-second-law-thermodynamics.html">Second Law of Thermodynamics</a>&nbsp;seems to work in a single direction. It talks about the natural tendency of any &ldquo;closed&rdquo; system to degenerate into a more disordered system. With this view, the implication is that the universe will ultimately end in a heat-death, in which everything is at the same temperature.</p>
<p>Dissipative systems work in the other direction; they create order where no order previously existed. Economies&nbsp;get ever-more complex, as businesses grow larger and more hierarchical in form, governments provide more services, and the number of different jobs filled by members of the economy proliferate.&nbsp;How do we explain this additional order?</p>
<p>According to&nbsp;<a href="http://people.clas.ufl.edu/ulan/files/Harmony.pdf">Ulanowicz</a>, the traditional focus of thermodynamics has been on states, rather than on the process of getting from one state to another. What is needed is a theory that is more focused on processes, rather than states. He writes,</p>
<blockquote><div class="quote_start"><div></div></div><div class="quote_end"><div></div></div><p>. &nbsp;. &nbsp;. the prevailing view of the second law is an oversimplified version of its true nature. Simply put, entropy is not&nbsp;entirely&nbsp;about disorder. Away from equilibrium, there is an obverse and largely unappreciated side to the second law that, in certain circumstances, mandates the creation of order.</p>
</blockquote>
<p><strong>We are observing the mandated creation of order</strong>. For example, the human body takes heat energy and transforms it to mechanical energy. There is a dualism to the entropy system that many have not stopped to appreciate. Instead of a trend toward heat death always being the overarching goal, systems have a two-way nature to them. Dissipative systems are able to grow until they reach a point called <em>self-organized criticality</em> or the &ldquo;critical point&rdquo;; then they shrink from inadequate energy flows.</p>
<p>In forests, this point of self-organized criticality comes when the growth of the tall trees starts blocking out the light to the shorter plants. As mentioned earlier, at that point the forest starts becoming more susceptible to forest fires.&nbsp;<a href="http://people.clas.ufl.edu/ulan/files/Harmony.pdf">Ulanowicz</a>&nbsp;shows that for ecosystems with more than 12 elements, there is quite a narrow &ldquo;window of viability.&rdquo;</p>
<div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_40625" style="width: 495px;"><a href="https://gailtheactuary.files.wordpress.com/2016/02/ulanowicz-window-of-viability.png" rel="attachment wp-att-40625"><img alt="Figure 8. Illustration of close clustering of ecosystems with more than 12 elements, indicating the narrow &quot;window of viability&quot; of such ecosystems. From " class="size-full wp-image-40625" src="https://gailtheactuary.files.wordpress.com/2016/02/ulanowicz-window-of-viability.png?w=640" style="height: 449px; width: 600px;" /></a><br />
<p class="wp-caption-text"><em>Figure 8. Illustration of close clustering of ecosystems with more than 12 elements, indicating the narrow &ldquo;window of viability&rdquo; of such ecosystems. From <a href="http://people.clas.ufl.edu/ulan/files/Harmony.pdf">Ulanowicz</a></em></p>
</div>
<p>If we look at world per capita energy consumption, it seems to indicate a very narrow &ldquo;window of viability&rdquo; as well.</p>
<div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_40627" style="width: 650px;"><a href="https://gailtheactuary.files.wordpress.com/2016/02/world-energy-consumption-per-capita-with-notes.png" rel="attachment wp-att-40627"><img alt="Figure 9. World energy consumption per capita, based on BP Statistical Review of World Energy 2105 data. Year 2015 estimate and notes by G. Tverberg." class="size-full wp-image-40627" src="https://gailtheactuary.files.wordpress.com/2016/02/world-energy-consumption-per-capita-with-notes.png?w=640&amp;h=384" style="width: 600px; height: 360px;" /></a><br />
<p class="wp-caption-text"><em>Figure 9. World energy consumption per capita, based on BP Statistical Review of World Energy 2105 data. Year 2015 estimate and notes by G. Tverberg.</em></p>
</div>
<p><strong>When we look at what happened in the world economy alongside the history of world energy consumption, we can see a pattern.</strong> Back prior to 1973, when oil was less than $30 per barrel, oil consumption and the economy grew rapidly. A lot of infrastructure (interstate highways, electric transmission lines, and pipelines) was added in this timeframe. The 1973-1974 price shock and related recession briefly brought energy consumption down.</p>
<p><strong>It wasn&rsquo;t until the restructuring of the economy in the late 1970s and early 1980s that energy consumption really came down. </strong>There were many changes made: cars became smaller and more fuel efficient; electricity production was changed from oil to other approaches, often nuclear; regulation of utilities was changed toward greater competition, thus discouraging building infrastructure unless it was absolutely essential.</p>
<p><strong>The drop in energy consumption after 1991 reflects the fall of the Former Soviet Union. </strong>The huge ramp-up in energy consumption after 2001 represents the effect of adding China (with all of its jobs and coal consumption) to the World Trade Organization. With this change, energy needs became permanently higher, if China was to have enough jobs for its people. Each small dip seems to represent a recession. Recently energy consumption seems to be down again. If we consider low consumption along with low commodity prices, it makes for a worrying situation. Are we approaching a major recession, or worse?</p>
<p><strong>If we think of the world economy relative to its critical point, the world economy has been near this point since 1981, but various things have pulled us out.</strong></p>
<p>One thing that has helped the economy is the extremely high interest rate (18%) implemented in 1981. This high interest rate pushed down fossil fuel usage at that time. It also gave interest rates a very long way to fall. Falling interest rates have a very favorable impact on the economy. They encourage greater lending and tend to raise the selling prices of stocks. The economy has received a favorable boost from falling interest rates for almost the entire period between 1981 and the present.</p>
<p>Other factors were important as well. The fall of the Soviet Union in 1991 bought the rest of the world a little time (and saved oil extraction for later); the addition of China to the World Trade Organization in 2001 added a great deal of cheap coal to the energy mix, helping to bring down energy costs. These low energy costs, plus all of the debt China was able to add, allowed energy consumption and the world economy to grow again&ndash;temporarily pulling the world away from the critical point.</p>
<p>In 2008, oil prices dropped very low. It was only with QE that interest rates could be brought very low, and commodity prices bounced back up to adequate levels. Now we are again faced with low prices. It looks as if we are again at the critical point, and thus the edge of collapse.</p>
<p><strong>Once a dissipative structure is past its critical point, Roddier says that what is likely to bring it down is an <em>avalanche of bifurcations</em>. In the case of an economy, these might be debt defaults.</strong></p>
<p>In a dissipative structure, both communication and stored information are important. Stored information, which is very close to technology, becomes very important when food is hard to find or energy is high cost to extract. When energy is low-cost to extract, practically anyone can find and make use of energy, so technology is less important.</p>
<p>Communication in an economy is done in various ways, including through the use of money and debt. Few people understand the extent to which debt can give false signals about future availability of energy flows. Thus, it is possible for an economy to build up to a very large size, with few realizing that this approach to building an economy is very similar to a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ponzi_scheme">Ponzi Scheme</a>. It can continue only as long as energy costs are extremely low, or debt is being rapidly added.</p>
<p>In theory, EROEI calculations (comparing energy produced by a device or energy product to fossil fuel energy consumed increasing this product) should communicate the &ldquo;value&rdquo; of a particular energy product. Unfortunately, this calculation is based the common misunderstanding of the nature of the physics problem that I mentioned at the beginning of the article. (This is also true for similar analyses, such as Lifecycle Analyses.) These calculations would communicate valuable information, if our problem were &ldquo;running out&rdquo; of fossil fuels, and if the way to mitigate this problem were to use fossil fuels as sparingly as possible. If our problem is rising debt levels, EROEI and similar calculations do nothing to show us how to mitigate the problem.</p>
<p><em><u><strong>If the economy collapses, it will collapse down to a lower sustainable level. Much of the world&rsquo;s infrastructure was built when oil could be extracted for $20 per barrel. That time is long gone. So, it looks like the world will need to collapse back to a level before fossil fuels&ndash;perhaps much before fossil fuels.</strong></u></em></p>
<p>If it is any consolation, Prof. Roddier says that once new economies begin to form again, the survivors after collapse will tend to be more co-operative. In fact, he offers this graphic.</p>
<div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_40630" style="width: 650px;"><a href="https://gailtheactuary.files.wordpress.com/2016/02/two-sides-of-the-critical-point.png" rel="attachment wp-att-40630"><img alt="Figure 10. F. Roddier view of what happens on the two sides of the critical point. From upcoming translation of his book, &quot;The Thermodynamics of Evolution.&quot;" class="size-full wp-image-40630" src="https://gailtheactuary.files.wordpress.com/2016/02/two-sides-of-the-critical-point.png?w=640&amp;h=247" style="width: 601px; height: 232px;" /></a><br />
<p class="wp-caption-text"><em>Figure 10. F. Roddier view of what happens on the two sides of the critical point. From upcoming translation of his book, &ldquo;The Thermodynamics of Evolution.&rdquo;</em></p>
</div>
<p>We know that if there are survivors, new economies will be likely. We don&rsquo;t know precisely what they will be like, except that they will be limited to using resources that are available at that time.</p>

    <p>This 'fixed technology' bullshit - making speculative but declarative statements about limitations predicated on the assumption of little or no change in the technology underpinning economic activity - is the hallmark of dilettante (journalistic) understanding of economics and of production.</p>
<p>Oil is the only energy source? For someone who claims to grok physics, the author has no fucking idea about technological change.</p>
<p>It's as stupid as asserting, circa 1850, that it is impossible for man to cover ground as fast as a horse <em>because man only has 2 legs</em>.</p>
<p>I've seen stuff that's almost as bad - like fucktards who think that in order to build an AI that will surpass human-level cognition, it will have to be constructed along the same lines as a human brain.</p>
<p>To understand why that's retarded... ask yourself: what is the right answer to <em>'How can I move <strong>much </strong>faster than a horse?</em>'</p>
<p>It is absolutely not "Grow two more legs, and a mane, and eat lots of grass."</p>
<p>The right answer is "<em>Dig up some special dirt, melt it, cool it, and press or mold it it into a variety of shapes, and put those shapees together into a bigger shape that looks nothing like a horse. Then dig up some brown ooze, refine it, put it in a receptacle within the new non-horse-shaped thing. Then progressively-explode the refined brown ooze under pressure, and capture the released energy through an ingenious system of cams and shafts. Use less-refined brown ooze to make sure that shit doesn't stick together when it gets hot.</em>"</p>
<p>Funnily enough, that's almost the same answer for "How do I fly faster than a falcon, and farther than a migrating goose?".</p>
<p>Answer: "<em>Dig up some different types of dirt, melt it, cool it, and make a tube out of it. Fill the tube with explosives refined from different ooze, and explode the refined ooze</em>."</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>USE TECHNOLOGY. INNOVATE.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Humans  are a now trechnological species, first and foremost. We understand  quite a lot about the planet we live on, and a fair bit the universe in which the  planet is situated. We understand quite a lot about the local (i.e.,  planetary, resource) constraints under which we operate, and we  understand quite a bit less about broader physical (in the sense of 'to  do with physics') constraints.</p>
<p>Even the most speculative  forms of theoretical physics is quite 'conservative' in what it will  accept: physics as we currently understand it, almost certainly assumes  more constraints than will eventually prove to be the case.</p>
<p>With that said... the amount of energy that is floating around uncaptured <em>by our current technology</em>, is staggering.</p>
<p>I  know this sounds New Age-y, and it annoys me that the internet abounds  with the (citation-free) assertion that Feynman and Wheeler 'calculated'  that there is sufficient energy in a seemingly-empty coffee cup, to  boil the world's oceans; usually this reference is made on hippy-dippy  sites plugging ZPE ('zero point energy') as something  currently-accessible (which it's not).</p>
<p>However one thing in  irrefutable: zero-point energy exists and is absolutely abundant. We  cannot currently harness it, but doing so would not violate any of the  known laws of physics - in other words it is not 'unharnessable'...  which means that we will learn how to harness it.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>In the same way, APM (<em>atomically-precise manufacture</em>) does not violate any known physical laws, and will, once implemented, reduce the energy requirements of manufacture by a factor of (roughly) ten thousand. So eventually it will be a thing, and the cost of manufactured goods will fall to near-zero.</p>
<p>Feynman gave a lecture on this in 1959 called "<a href="http://www.zyvex.com/nanotech/feynman.html" rel="nofollow"></a><em><a href="http://www.zyvex.com/nanotech/feynman.html" rel="nofollow"><strong>There's PLENTY of Room at the Bottom</strong></a></em>" - he forecast integrated circuits in that lecture, and guessed that you should be able to etch the contents of an Encyclopaedia on the head of a pin without serious technological challenge (and by using a 7-bit encoding, you could fit all of the book in the world in a cube a quarter-inch across).</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>We will have APM; we will have Strong AI (which will arise from something that is radically different from a human brain in both its structure and its function); we will have zero-point energy (not in a hippy-dippy way); we will go past the Singularity - and we will do all those things in my lifetime.</p>
<p>Literally the only caveat to this, is that the parasite political class might weaponise the technology first. This is why they have to be got rid of - non-violently if possible, but violently if necessary.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>And OIL? Who the fuck cares? It's not going to matter a fucking iota within a generation; we will be dragging CO<sub>2</sub> out of the atmosphere in order to recover the carbon to build shit with (by the by - that's why 'carbon emissions' would not be a problem even if it's true that human carbon emissions cause climate change: but it's not true anyhow).</p>
