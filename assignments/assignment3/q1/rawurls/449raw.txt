&#8220;Until now, the trend has been to take a tiny region in the brain and learn 
  everything about that cube of tissue. But if you don&#8217;t know where synapses 
  are coming from, you can&#8217;t do anything with some of that information,&#8221; said 
  Barth. 
</p>
<p/>
<p>
The researchers dug up an older EM staining technique using ethanolic 
  phosphotungstic acid (EPTA), which only highlights synapses. They also 
  developed a computer algorithm to search for the synapses in EM images. 
  After selectively clipping whiskers of young mice to change how they learned 
  about their environment, Barth and colleagues then analyzed shifts in 
  synapse density and length during critical developmental stages. 
</p>
<p/>
<p>
The method allows for a broad view of synaptic changes in regions of the brain 
  because it can process a breathtaking number of images quickly. Using the 
  technique, the researchers analyzed almost 25,000 images containing 40,000 
  synapses. 
</p>
<p/>
<p>
&#8220;I love the idea that we could collect a ton of data and use that to drive our 
  hypotheses,&#8221; said Barth. &#8220;The extent that we could look at coordinated 
  changes means we can develop much better hypotheses.&#8221;
</p>
<p/>
<p>
The team found some surprising increases and decreases in synapse density in 
  some brain areas, a finding they will explore further with other techniques 
  that can capture details at the cellular level. Barth also intends to 
  develop a way to selectively label different types of synapses since their 
  method is currently unable to distinguish one type of synapse from another. 
</p>
