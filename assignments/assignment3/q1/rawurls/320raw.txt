											<div class="news-item-main-img" style="background-image: url('https://content.careeronecdn.com.au/g1x/girls_in_shirts_william_west_afp_getty_images.jpg');" ></div>

						<p class="caption">One take on relaxed attire. Pic: WILLIAM WEST/AFP/Getty Images</p>
										<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">ON Thursday, Racing Victoria launches its</span><em><span style="font-weight: 400;"> Relaxed Racing</span></em><span style="font-weight: 400;"> initiative at Caulfield.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The </span><em><span style="font-weight: 400;">Relaxed Racing</span></em><span style="font-weight: 400;"> homepage tells us this is the &ldquo;breezy little sister of the Spring Racing Carnival&rdquo;, running from February through March. A time to hit the track with the family and enjoy the casual vibe, maybe a picnic, some tunes and, if you&rsquo;re lucky, a winner or two.</span></p>
<p>Brilliant.</p>
<p>But what I want to know is this - why can&rsquo;t we relax at the races all year round and why can&rsquo;t racing shake off its stuffy, outdated dress codes for the rest of the year?</p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Everything that </span><em><span style="font-weight: 400;">Relaxed Racing</span></em><span style="font-weight: 400;">&nbsp;promotes is what we should be spruiking to potential patrons every week of the year, not just as a summertime gimmick.</span></p>
<p>We keep hearing from racing administrators that the sport needs to evolve with the needs of modern audiences, so why continue to push stodgy dress codes for the majority of the year in an era when the average office worker doesn&rsquo;t even wear a suit to work?</p>
<p>I applaud Racing Victoria for continuing with the initiative and I point to the three city clubs (MRC, MVRC, VRC) for offering relaxed members' dress codes during summer (jackets and ties optional at MRC &amp; MVRC, jackets optional but ties required at VRC). But surely we&rsquo;ve reached a point as a society in 2016 that we do not require patrons of a sporting event to dress as if they&rsquo;re attending a funeral.</p>
<p>But Shark, I hear you say, if you don&rsquo;t want to wear a suit and tie just go to the public areas.</p>
<p>Great! That means there will be five people on the public lawn instead of four!</p>
<p>Standing on an abandoned concrete carpark is not my idea of a good time or what I pay my club membership fees for. I, and many like me, want to enjoy the members' facilities we pay for as well a recreational day at the track in comfortable clobber.</p>
<p>I don&rsquo;t want to be treated like a second class citizen and turned away at the barriers because I&rsquo;m not wearing a suit and tie.</p>
<p>Look at the picnic racing model and the explosion of attendance at rural and seaside tracks around Victoria; I can tell you that the crowds aren&rsquo;t there to spot the next Black Caviar, rather they&nbsp;enjoy a day at the races with friends and family without the fuss and bother of getting dressed to the nines.</p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Racing continues to struggle in its attempts to attract and maintain new fans and continues to preach to an ageing audience. Sports betting continues to siphon current and potential racing punters away from the sport in droves. Off-season race track crowds, in public or members' areas, are reminiscent of the daytime scenes in the post-apocalyptic flick </span><em><span style="font-weight: 400;">I Am Legend</span></em><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The requirement for suits and ties outside of the designated </span><em><span style="font-weight: 400;">Relaxed Racing</span></em><span style="font-weight: 400;"> timeslot, is not helping racing fight back.</span></p>
<p>So tomorrow, the head honchos will tell us that it&rsquo;s time to relax, but come the end of March it&rsquo;s back to the nonsensical and archaic practice of suiting up for a leisure pursuit each weekend.</p>
<p>Let&rsquo;s give racing a makeover. Sharkie says: Relax...all year round.</p>
