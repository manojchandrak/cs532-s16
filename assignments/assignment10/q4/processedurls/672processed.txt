Flo benefits from military training

   News Wed 10 Feb 2016

   Chelsea legend, ambassador and Academy coach Tore Andre Flo tested his
   resilience to the limit recently as part of a Premier League initiative
   for top-level coaches.

   Flo, who represented the Blues between 1997 and 2000, famously netting
   a hat-trick at White Hart Lane in a 6-1 win, was one of 16 16 elite
   young coaches invited on the trip to the Brecon Beacons in Wales to
   gain an understanding of the military approach to leadership, teamwork
   and communication, making them better equipped to fulfil the Elite
   Player Performance Plan remit of developing more elite home-grown
   players. The initiative is part of the Elite Coaching Apprentice
   Scheme.

Premier League?s Elite Coach Apprentice Scheme

   29/01/2016 Duration: 1m 53s

   ‘The weekend was very challenging but very enjoyable – especially when
   it was finished,’ Flo said. ‘It was very tough - very tiring, very cold
   – but it was good to be stretched physically and mentally.

   ‘Overnight, I was outside in a sleeping bag, overnight. It was minus
   one and there was wind, snow, rain and then clear skies. We had all
   weathers in one night. In our rucksacks, we had tiny mats underneath
   our sleeping bags but we basically slept on the muddy ground.

   ‘We were soldiers in a war situation. We used paintball bullets but the
   guns looked real enough. Sometimes we had to go into mountains and
   rescue a pilot who had crash-landed. For one part, we were in a medical
   team and had to carry off the wounded on stretchers. Then we were in a
   hostage situation and had to negotiate. We cooked our military rations
   on a tiny stove. I did not sleep at all on the first night.

   ‘We learned a lot about how important it is to communicate in
   situations like that and to take decisions very clearly. These are
   aspects I can take back into my coaching because they are also very
   important in football. That was a theme throughout the weekend,
   leadership. Mentally it was very tough.’

   The coaches were given a range of military tasks and rotated in the
   different roles. The organisers were looking for them to understand and
   apply dynamic and effective leadership skills; function as part of a
   high-performance team; plan and deal with tasks and problems
   effectively; and employ the communication skills required to lead or
   work within the team.

   Through a series of assignments and challenges ranging from learning
   from the Royal Shakespeare Company in Stratford-upon-Avon to gleaning
   communication skills off Alistair Campbell, previously Tony Blair’s PR
   aide, the coaches work towards their ECAS certificate.
