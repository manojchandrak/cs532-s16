        <p><strong>NEW DELHI </strong>-- Millions of Indian children are getting deworming treatment in a massive national campaign to prevent parasitic worms from infecting them and impairing their mental and physical development.</p><p>In a village on the outskirts of the Indian capital, the children washed their hands and lined up to chew the deworming tablets given to them by their teachers and health workers before opening their bags for studies on Wednesday morning.</p><p><figure class="image pull-none image-large "><span class="img "><img src="http://cbsnews1.cbsistatic.com/hub/i/r/2016/02/10/297e5fd7-71df-4c68-9151-2e01f6a0b33c/thumbnail/620x350/5da0f3d8a8e050210dc098ea12a2e592/ap976324575584.jpg" alt="ap976324575584.jpg" height="350" width="620" srcset="http://cbsnews1.cbsistatic.com/hub/i/r/2016/02/10/297e5fd7-71df-4c68-9151-2e01f6a0b33c/thumbnail/620x350/5da0f3d8a8e050210dc098ea12a2e592/ap976324575584.jpg 1x, http://cbsnews1.cbsistatic.com/hub/i/r/2016/02/10/297e5fd7-71df-4c68-9151-2e01f6a0b33c/thumbnail/1240x700/5f653bbd9d77806f1eca45ace5e00381/ap976324575584.jpg 2x"></span><figcaption><div class="caption">A primary class student takes a deworming pill as others wait in a queue at a school in Neemrana, 76.8 miles from New Delhi, in the Indian state of Rajsthan Wednesday, Feb. 10, 2016.</div><div class="credit">
                                            Manish Swarup, AP
                                        </div></figcaption></figure></p><p>The campaign is targeting 270 million children across the country for Wednesday's effort and another next week for children missed the first time, the Health Ministry said in a statement.</p><p>Worms interfere with nutrient uptake in children and could lead to anemia, malnourishment and physical and mental impairment. The infection risk is mainly caused by lack of proper toilets, unclean drinking water, poor hygiene and dirty surroundings.</p><p>The World Health Organization estimates that 220 million children between the ages of 1 and 14 are at risk of parasitic worm infection in India.</p><p>On Wednesday, nearly 900,000 teachers, principals and health workers were mobilized in schools and government-sponsored child and mother care centers.</p><p>The deworming initiative, along with measures to address hygiene and sanitation, would  go a long way in improving the health of children as well as that of the country, said By Poonam Khetrapal Singh, the WHO Regional Director for South-East Asia.</p><p><figure class="image pull-none image-large "><span class="img "><img src="http://cbsnews1.cbsistatic.com/hub/i/r/2016/02/10/71fa453b-3b91-42a7-a826-f17267d01b01/thumbnail/620x350/d4946b02a1acc9f0e1c9fdac57527e3a/ap715537308897.jpg" alt="ap715537308897.jpg" height="350" width="620" srcset="http://cbsnews1.cbsistatic.com/hub/i/r/2016/02/10/71fa453b-3b91-42a7-a826-f17267d01b01/thumbnail/620x350/d4946b02a1acc9f0e1c9fdac57527e3a/ap715537308897.jpg 1x, http://cbsnews2.cbsistatic.com/hub/i/r/2016/02/10/71fa453b-3b91-42a7-a826-f17267d01b01/thumbnail/1240x700/9a6b4e0a2e4e82c49451ab1e3519f1d8/ap715537308897.jpg 2x"></span><figcaption><div class="caption">Indian schoolchildren line up to take a deworming tablet in Hyderabad, India, Wednesday, Feb. 10, 2016.</div><div class="credit">
                                            Mahesh Kumar A., AP
                                        </div></figcaption></figure></p><p>In a statement, Singh said India's campaign complements WHO's battle against neglected tropical diseases, those on the verge of elimination that continue to be public health challenges.</p><p>Priya Jha, who leads non-governmental organization Evidence Action's work in India, said details of a second dose to the children would be decided soon by the Indian government on the basis of a study carried out in different states.</p><p>She said the campaign went off smoothly with children and their parents eager to participate in it. The tablets were provided by the WHO and the Indian government.</p>
